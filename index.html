c. 2700-2100 BCE

Dancing Girl of Harappa

Indus Valley Civilization

Location: National Museum, New Delhi

This bronze figurine represents one of the earliest known examples of metal casting in the Indian subcontinent. Discovered in Harappa, it showcases the sophisticated metallurgical skills of the Indus Valley people.

c. 250 BCE

Lion Capital of Ashoka

Mauryan Empire

Location: Sarnath Museum (replica); Original adopted as India's National Emblem

Created during Emperor Ashoka's reign, this capital topped one of his famous pillars and represents the spread of Buddhism across the empire. Ashoka adopted Buddhism halfway through his 40-year reign and patronized Buddhist art.

c. 2nd century BCE

Sanchi Stupa

Mauryan to Gupta periods

Location: Sanchi, Madhya Pradesh

Built during Ashoka's reign and expanded over several centuries, Sanchi represents the evolution of Buddhist architecture and sculpture. It's considered the oldest stone structure in India.

c. 2nd century BCE - 6th century CE

Ajanta Cave Paintings

Mauryan to Gupta periods

Location: Ajanta, Maharashtra

Created over several centuries by Buddhist monks, these cave paintings represent the golden age of Indian art. The Gupta period is considered the classic peak of north Indian art.

c. 10th-12th century CE

Nataraja Bronze Sculpture

Chola Dynasty

Location: Various museums; National Museum, New Delhi has notable examples

Created during the Chola period, known for bronze sculptures and the lost-wax casting technique. The Chola dynasty was prolific in their pursuit of arts and temple construction.

c. 1700-1800 CE

Tipu Sultan's Personal Sword

Mysore Sultanate

Location: National Museum, New Delhi

Belonged to Tipu Sultan, the ruler of Mysore during the late 18th century, who fought against British colonial expansion. Represents the synthesis of Islamic and Indian craftsmanship.

1905 CE

Bharat Mata

Bengal School of Art

Location: Various collections

Created during the Indian independence movement, this painting became a symbol of nationalism and resistance against British rule. Tagore founded the Bengal School of Art as a reaction against Western academic art.
